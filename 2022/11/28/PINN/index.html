PINN

Posted on 2022-11-28 In Paper Reading

Basic Information

  • Title: Physics-informed neural networks: A deep learning framework for solving forward and inverse problems involving nonlinear partial differential equations
  • Authors: Raissi et al.
  • Pub Date: 2021-6
  • Journal/Conference: Journal of Computational Physics

Background & Motivation

Background

  • in the course of analyzing complex physical, biological or engineering systems, the cost of data acquisition is prohibitive, and we are inevitably faced with the challenge of drawing conclusions and making decisions under partial information
  • there exists a vast amount of prior knowledge that is currently not being utilized in modern machine learning practice

Motivation

  • use prior information as a regularization agent that constrains the space of admissible solutions to a manageable size

Main Contributions of This Work

  • use automatic differentiation to differentiate neural networks with respect to their input coordinates and model parameters

Main Difference between This Work & Prior Ones

  • Prior Ones: employ machine learning algorithms merely as black-box tools
  • This Work: open the black-box by understanding and appreciating the key role played by automatic differentiation

Main Techniques & Experiments

Problems Setup

$$
u_t + \mathcal{N}[u; \lambda] =0,\ x \in \Omega,\ t \in [0,T]
$$

  • $u(t,x)$: the latent (hidden) solution
  • $\mathcal{N}[\cdot;\lambda]$: nonlinear operator parameterized by $\lambda$
  • $\Omega$: subset of $\mathbb{R}^D$

Two Problems:

  1. data-driven solutions of PDEs: given fixed model parameters $\lambda$ what can be said about the unknown hidden state $u(t, x)$ of the system?
  2. data-driven discovery of PDEs: what are the parameters λ that best describe the observed data?

Data-Driven Solutions of PDEs

Continuous Time Model

Define $f(t, x)$ as follows:
$$
f:=u_t + \mathcal{N}[u]
$$
We can use a deep neural network to represent $u(t, x)$. This network can be derived using automatic differentiation. In the meanwhile, it has the same parameters as $f$.

The shared parameters between $u$ and $f$ can be learned by minimizing the MSE loss:
$$
\mathrm{MSE} = \mathrm{MSE}_u + \mathrm{MSE}_f
$$
in which
$$
\mathrm{MSE}u = \frac{1}{N_u}\sum{i=1}^{N_u} |u(t_u^i, x_u^i) - u^i|^2
$$

  • ${t_u^i, x_u^i, u^i}^{N_u}_{i=1}$: initial and boundary training data on $u(t,x)$
  • corresponds to the initial and boundary data

$$
\mathrm{MSE}f = \frac{1}{N_f}\sum{i=1}^{N_f} |f(t_f^i, x_f^i)|^2
$$

  • ${t_f^i, x_f^i}^{N_f}_{i=1}$: collocations points for $f(t,x)$
  • enforces the structure imposed by PDE at a finite set of collocation points

Example: Schrodinger equation
$$
\begin{aligned}
&ih_t + 0.5h_{xx} + |h|^2h = 0, x \in [-5, 5], t \in [0, \pi/2],\
&h(t, -5) = h(t, 5), h_x(t, -5) = h_x(t, 5),\
&h(0, x) = 2 \mathrm{sech} (x)
\end{aligned}
$$
Let’s define:
$$
f := ih_t + 0.5h_{xx} + |h|^2h
$$
in which $h(t,x) = [u(t, x), v(t, x)]$. For loss:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{MSE} &= \mathrm{MSE}_0 + \mathrm{MSE}_b + \mathrm{MSE}_f,\
\mathrm{MSE}0 &= \frac{1}{N_0} \sum{i=1}^{N_0} |h(0, x_0^i) -h_0^i|^2,\
\mathrm{MSE}b &= \frac{1}{N_b} \sum{i=1}^{N_b} \left( |h^i(t_b^i, -5) -h^i(t_b^i, 5) |^2 + |h_x^i(t_b^i, -5) -h_x^i(t_b^i, 5) |^2 \right),\
\mathrm{MSE}f &= \frac{1}{N_f} \sum{i=1}^{N_f} |f(t_f^i, x_f^i)|^2,
\end{aligned}
$$

Discrete Time Model

Let us apply the general form of Runge–Kutta methods with q stages:
$$
\begin{aligned}
u^{n+c_i} &= u^n - \Delta t \sum_{j=1}^q a_{ij} \mathcal{N}[u^{n+c_j}], \quad i = 1, \dots, q,\
u^{n+1} &= u^n - \Delta t \sum_{j=1}^q b_j \mathcal{N}[u^{n+c_j}].
\end{aligned}
$$
Last equation can be equivalently expressed as
$$
\begin{aligned}
u^n &= u^n_i, \quad i = 1, \dots, q,\
u^n &= u^n_{q+1}
\end{aligned}
$$
where
$$
\begin{aligned}
u^n_i &:= u^{n+c_i} + \Delta t \sum_{j=1}^q a_{ij} \mathcal{N}[u^{n+c_j}]\
u^n_{q+1} &:= u^{n+1} + \Delta t \sum_{j=1}^q b_j \mathcal{N}[u^{n+c_j}]
\end{aligned}
$$
We proceed by placing a multi-output neural network prior on
$$
[u^{n+c_1}(x), \dots, u^{n+c_q}(x), u^{n+1}(x)]
$$
This prior assumption along with discrete PDE result in a physics-informed neural network that takes x as an input and outputs
$$
[u^n_1(x), \dots, u_q^n(x), u_{q+1}^n(x)]
$$
Example: Allen–Cahn equation
$$
\begin{aligned}
&u_t - 0.0001u_{xx} + 5u^3 - 5u = 0,\
&u(0, x) = x^2 \cos(\pi x),\
&u(t,-1) = u(t, 1),\
&u_x(t, -1) = u_x(t, 1).
\end{aligned}
$$
Here
$$
\mathcal{N}[u^{n+c_j}] = -0.0001 u_{xx}^{n+c_j} + 5(u^{n+c_j})^3 - 5u^{n+c_j}
$$
and
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{SSE} &= \mathrm{SSE}n + \mathrm{SSE}b\
\mathrm{SSE}n &= \sum{j=1}^{q+1} \sum{i=1}^{N_n}|u{j}^n(x^{n,i}) - u^{n,i}|^2\
\mathrm{SSE}b &= \sum{i=1}^q |u^{n+c_i}(-1) - u^{n+c_i}(1) |^2 + |u^{n+1}(-1) - u^{n+1}(1) |^2\
&+ \sum_{i=1}^q |u^{n+c_i}_x(-1) - u^{n+c_i}_x(1) |^2 + |u^{n+1}_x(-1) - u^{n+1}_x(1) |^2
\end{aligned}
$$

Data-Driven Discovery of PDEs

  • The whole process is similar to data-driven solution of PDEs.
  • generate data set from exact solution, then according to MSE loss mentioned above, optimize and get unknown parameters

Advantages & Shortcomings

Advantages

  • use neural networks as the PDEs’ solution approximator, which have excellent performance in nonlinear function approximation.
  • first use auto differential tools into computational physics creatively, and make the whole model partially explainable
  • use customized loss function as soft penalty to lead the training process of neural networks, which make it can be generalized to more problems

Shortcomings

  • the accuracy is still very large ($10^{-3}$)
  • this kind of method still remains inexplicability

Although a series of promising results was presented, the reader may perhaps agree this work creates more questions than it answers.

  • How deep/wide should the neural network be?
  • How much data is really needed?
  • Why does the algorithm converge to unique values for the parameters of the differential operators, i.e., why is the algorithm not suffering from local optima for the parameters of the differential operator?
  • Does the network suffer from vanishing gradients for deeper architectures and higher order differential operators?
  • Could this be mitigated by using different activation functions?
  • Can we improve on initializing the network weights or normalizing the data?
  • Are the mean square error and the sum of squared errors the appropriate loss functions? Why are these methods seemingly so robust to noise in the data?
  • How can we quantify the uncertainty associated with our predictions?
