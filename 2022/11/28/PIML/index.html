Physics-Informed Machine Learning

Posted on 2022-11-28 In Paper Reading

Basic Information

  • Title: Physics Informed Machine Learning
  • Authors: Karniadakis et al.
  • Pub Date: 2021-6
  • Journal/Conference: Nature Reviews Physics

Introduction

Background

  • Main theme: Modeling and forecasting the dynamics of multiphysics and multiscale systems

  • Methods Now: numerically solving PDEs, such as using finite differences, finite elements, spectral and mesh methods

  • Problems:

    • solving inverse problems (inferring material properties in functional materials or discovering missing physics in reactive transport)
    • solving real-life physical problems with missing, gappy, or noisy boundary conditions

ML

  • Background: Observation data can play an important role and a wealth of multi-fidelity observations is ready thanks to trillions of sensors
  • Advantages of ML: ML can explore massive design spaces, identity multi-dimensional correlations, and manage ill-posed problems
    • Especially, DL approaches can extract features from massive amounts of multi-fidelity observational data
  • Problems of ML:
    • unable to extract interpretable information and knowledge from data
    • extrapolation or observational biases may lead to poor generalization performance

Physics Informed Machine Learning

  • Background:

    • the ability to collect and create observational data far outpaces the ability to assimilate it sensibly, let alone understand it

    • We need methods to integrate fundamental physical laws and domain knowledge by teaching ML models about governing physical rules

  • Motivation:

    • prior knowledge stemming from our observational, empirical, physical, or mathematical understanding of the world can be leveraged to improve the performance of a learning algorithm
    • yield more interpretable ML methods that remain robust in presence of imperfect data

  • Advantages of PINN:

    • PINN integrates seamlessly data and mathematical physics models, even in partially understood, uncertain, and high-dimensional contexts
    • Kernel-based or neural network-based regression methods offer effective, simple, and meshless implementations
    • PINNs are effective and efficient for ill-posed and inverse problems, and combined with domain decomposition are scalable to large problems

How to Embed Physics in ML

Observational Biases

  • introduced through data that embody the underlying physics or carefully crafted data augmentation procedures
  • Problem: a lot of data is needed to reinforce biases and generate predictions that respect certain symmetries and conservation laws, which may be very difficult to get

Inductive Biases

  • design specialized NN architectures that embed prior knowledge, typically expressed in the form of certain mathematical constraints.
  • Examples:
    • CV tasks involving medical images, climate pattern segmentation, and others based on CNNs (generalized to symmetry transformations)
    • covariant NNs tailored to conform with the rotation and translation invariances present in many body systems
    • other built-in blocks: anti-symmetry, Galilean invariance, the symplectic structure of the underlying Hamiltonian system
    • special modified architectures to satisfy exactly the required initial conditions
  • Problem: may be limited to accounting for relatively simple symmetry groups

Learning Biases

  • introduced by appropriate choice of loss functions, constraints, and inference algorithms
  • Examples: Deep Galerkin Method, PINNs, constrained GAN, physics-informed auto-encoders (preserve Lyapunov stability), InvNet
  • solutions obtained via optimization with such soft penalty constraints and regularization can be viewed as equivalent to the maximum a-posteriori estimate of a Bayesian formulation stemming from physics-based likelihood assumptions

Connections to Kernel Methods & Classical Numerical Methods

  • Connections to Kernel Methods:
    • NN methods can be interpreted as kernel methods in which the underlying warping kernel is also learned from data
    • analyzing NN models through the lens of kernel methods could have considerable benefits
  • Connections to Classical Numerical Methods:
    • many modern deep learning models can be viewed and analyzed by observing an obvious correspondence and specific connections to many of these classical algorithms
    • Examples:
      • CNNs —— finite different stencils in translationally equivariant PDE discretizations
      • residual NNs —— basic forward Euler discretization of autonomous ordinary differential equations
      • DNNs with the ReLU activation function —— continuous piecewise linear functions from the linear finite-element method

Merits of Physics-Informed ML

  • PINNs: solving ill-posed and inverse problems
  • existing numerical grid-based solvers: forward, well-posed problems that do not require any data assimilation

Incomplete Models and Imperfect Data

  • it is possible to find meaningful solutions even when the problem is not perfectly well posed
  • when dealing with imperfect models and data, it is beneficial to integrate the Bayesian approach with physics-informed learning for uncertainty quantification
  • physics-informed learning is mesh-free, without computationally expensive mesh generation

Strong generalization in small data regime

  • physics-informed learning has the advantage of strong generalization in the small data regime
  • to enforce the physics, one can embed the physical principles into the network architecture, use physics as soft penalty constraints or use data augmentation

Understanding Deep Learning

  • physical principles are also being used to provide theoretical insight and elucidate the inner mechanisms behind the surprising effectiveness of deep learning

  • Shallow NNs can also be viewed as interacting particle systems and hence can be analyzed in the probability measure space with mean-field theory, instead of the high-dimensional parameter space

Tackling High Dimensionality

  • DNNs can break the curse of dimensionality under the condition that the target function is a hierarchical composition of local functions
  • GANs can be used to quantify parametric uncertainty in high-dimensional stochastic differential equations
  • deep operator networks can tackle the curse of dimensionality associated with the input space

Uncertainty quantification

  • Categories: from physics, from data, from learning models

Applications

  • Main Usage: inverse and ill-posed problems

Examples

  • Flow over an espresso cup
  • Physics-informed deep learning for 4D-flow MRI
  • Studying transitions between metastable states of a distribution
  • Application to quantum chemistry
  • Thermodynamically consistent PINNs
  • Application to material sciences
  • Application to molecular simulations

Software

Which Model?

PINNs are used to infer a deterministic function that is compatible with an underlying physical law when a limited number of observations is available

  • MLP: generally applicable but does not encode any specialized inductive biases
  • CNN: suitable for gridded 2D domains
  • Fourier feature network: suitable for PDEs whose solution exhibits high frequencies or periodic boundaries
  • Recurrent Architectures: suitable for non-Markovian and time-discrete problems

Current Limitations

  • Multiscale and multiphysics problems
  • New algorithms and computational frameworks
  • Data generation and benchmarks
  • New mathematics

Future Directions

  • Digital twins
  • Data and model transformations, fusion, and interpretability
  • Searching for intrinsic variables and emergent, useful representations

Ultimately, the main element we see changing is what we mean by ‘understanding’. Up to now, understanding meant that, say, each term in a PDE had a physical or mechanistic interpretation operated on some physically meaningful observables (dependent variables) and also operated in terms of some physically meaningful space/time (independent) variables. Now, it becomes possible to make accurate predictions without this type of mechanistic understanding — and ‘understanding’ is something that may be redefined in the process.
